🛡️ HOME SOC IDS: Administrator Operations Manual
Version: 8.0 (Diurnal AI & Peer Clustering Edition)
System Purpose: A Python-driven, AI-powered intrusion detection system built to secure home networks by analyzing DNS telemetry and network flow behavior.

1. System Architecture Overview
The Home IDS operates as a real-time, stateful stream-processing engine. It does not simply look at individual packets; it builds "memories" of how devices behave and compares current behavior against historical norms.

The architecture is divided into five sequential layers:

Ingestion Layer:

Pi-hole FTL Collector: Reads DNS queries directly from the local SQLite WAL (Write-Ahead Log) database every 2 seconds without blocking Pi-hole's writing process.

Zeek Log Tailer: Monitors live network flow protocols (conn.log, notice.log, weird.log) to catch threats that bypass DNS (e.g., hardcoded IP malware).

State & Memory Layer (state.py):

Maintains Exponentially Weighted Moving Averages (EWMA) for every device on the network.

Splits baseline tracking into Day (06:00–22:00) and Night (22:00–06:00) matrices.

Feature Extraction Layer (features.py):

Translates raw packet data into statistical metrics (Entropy, Ratios, Z-Scores) over a rolling 5-minute window.

Threat Intel & AI Layer (threat_intel.py, ml_engine.py):

Static Analysis: Checks OTX, AbuseIPDB, VirusTotal, and Abuse.ch for known bad actors.

Behavioral Analysis: Feeds the metrics into a per-device IsolationForest machine learning model to detect zero-day or stealth anomalies.

Scoring & Alerting Layer (scoring.py, alerts.py):

Calculates a final Risk Score (0.0 to 10.0).

Dispatches payloads to local JSON logs and the Telegram bot if the alert threshold is breached.

2. Core Operating Principles
The "Safe Corridor" Concept
The system uses statistical standard deviation (Z-Scores) to create a dynamic "Safe Corridor" for every device. An IoT smart plug and a gaming PC have vastly different traffic volumes. The IDS calculates the mean (average) query rate and the variance for each specific device by time of day. A threat is only triggered when a device violates its own physical history, not a global network average.

Cold Start & Peer Clustering
When a brand-new device joins the network (or after a system wipe), it has no history. The IDS places it under a Probationary Guard (relying on absolute limits) while simultaneously checking the Peer Cluster Registry. It will temporarily grade the new device against the established baseline of similar device types (e.g., grading a new iPhone against the baseline of existing iPhones) until it collects 100 localized data points.

Anti-Poisoning Guardrails
If a device is infected with malware the moment it joins the network, the malware's traffic would normally become part of the device's "baseline," rendering the IDS blind. The Anti-Poisoning Guardrail freezes the Machine Learning pipeline and baseline updates instantly if a device hits a severe risk threshold (> 7.0) or matches a deterministic threat intel hash, ensuring the model never learns malicious behavior as "normal."

3. Threat Hunting Playbook: Signal Combinations
The true power of this script lies in combinations. Individual alerts can sometimes be false positives, but when specific metrics spike together, it reveals the exact nature of the malware.

Use this playbook when looking at your Grafana Dashboard or analyzing a Telegram alert.

🔴 Scenario A: Ransomware or DGA Burst
The Concept: Malware families use Domain Generation Algorithms (DGAs) to guess thousands of randomized, gibberish domains (e.g., xkqztp.ru, mzblqw.ru) hoping one is an active command server.
The Combination to Look For:

High New Domains (Spike): The device is querying dozens of domains it has never seen before.

High NXDOMAIN Ratio (> 0.4): Most of the DGA guesses fail because the domains don't exist.

High DGA Z-Score: The entropy (randomness of the letters) triggers the mathematical heuristic.

High TLD Concentration: The malware is concentrating all its guesses on a specific Top Level Domain (like .ru, .xyz, or .cc).

Action: Disconnect the device immediately. Ransomware is actively attempting to pull its encryption keys.

🔴 Scenario B: Command & Control (C2) Beaconing
The Concept: A compromised device has successfully connected to a hacker's infrastructure and is "checking in" silently every few minutes waiting for a command.
The Combination to Look For:

Elevated Risk Velocity Z-Score (> 3.0): The device's risk score suddenly jumped relative to its own baseline, even if the absolute volume is low.

Zeek JA3 Malicious Hit (> 0): The TLS handshake matches known offensive frameworks like Cobalt Strike, Sliver, or Metasploit.

High Geo Beaconing Count: Persistent, rhythmic communication to a specific, unusual country or ASN.

Elevated Top Domain Ratio: The device is abandoning normal browsing rhythms and focusing heavily on one single destination.

Action: Check the "Top Beaconing ASNs" table in Grafana to identify the exact IP/Domain owning the C2 channel.

🔴 Scenario C: DNS Tunnelling (Data Exfiltration)
The Concept: Attackers are bypassing your firewall by encoding stolen data into the subdomains of DNS requests (e.g., password-file-chunk-1.attacker.com).
The Combination to Look For:

High Deep Domains Count (> 0): Domains with more than 5 sub-labels are extremely rare in legitimate traffic.

Extreme Entropy Z-Score: The subdomains are heavily encrypted/base64-encoded strings, causing maximum randomness.

High Unique Domains Count: Every chunk of stolen data requires a completely unique domain string.

Action: Check the raw DNS logs for the specific base domain receiving the deep subdomain queries and block it at the Pi-hole level.

🔴 Scenario D: Stealth Malware / Living-off-the-Land
The Concept: The attacker is using legitimate tools (like Windows Update or Google Drive) to hide their tracks. Z-Scores look normal, volume looks normal, and Pi-hole blocks are at 0%.
The Combination to Look For:

Elevated ML Anomaly Score (> 0.08): The IsolationForest AI engine has detected a structural shifting inside the multi-feature data matrix that human rules missed.

Flat / Normal Z-Scores: The standard deviations are quiet.

Action: The AI sees something humans can't. Look at the device's Zeek conn.log to see if it is making direct IP connections (bypassing DNS entirely) via the Zeek New IPs metric.

🔴 Scenario E: Internal Network Scanning / Worm Propagation
The Concept: An infected IoT device or laptop is trying to spread laterally across your home network.
The Combination to Look For:

High Suspicious Ports Count: Outbound connections attempting to reach ports 4444 (Metasploit), 31337 (Backdoors), or standard SMB/SSH ports on internal peers.

Spike in Zeek Protocol Anomalies: A surge in notice.log and weird.log events as the scanner throws malformed packets at your other devices.

Action: Isolate the infected device to a guest VLAN to prevent lateral infection.
